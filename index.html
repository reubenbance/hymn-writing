Building a Melody: Phrase Structure

Writing tunes that feel balanced and singable

Every good hymn tune is built out of small, organised chunks of music called phrases. Before you start writing our tune, it helps to understand how phrases are normally grouped, because almost every hymn follows the same basic blueprint.

4-bar phrases 8-bar sentences 16-bar tunes repetition cadences

1. The 4-Bar Phrase

The basic building block of almost all hymn tunes is the 4-bar phrase: a short musical idea, a like a single line of text. It usually ends with some sense of pause or punctuation, even if it's not a full stop.

Singer's test: if you can sing a 4-bar phrase in one comfortable breath, it's probably the right length.

2. The 8-Bar Sentence

Two 4-bar phrases joined together make an 8-bar musical sentence. This is the standard length of a pair of lines in a hymn verse. The first phrase usually feels "open" (like a comma), and the second phrase feels "closed" (like a full stop).

Why this works: the singers hear something familiar (the repeated opening), which makes the tune easy to learn, but the changed ending stops it feeling like a loop, and gives the verse a sense of arrival.

4. Cadences

A cadence is the musical equivalent of punctuation: it tells the singer whether a phrase is finished or not.

TypeFeels likeWhere to use it
Full / perfect cadenceA full stop: settled, resolvedEnd of the whole tune; often end of line 2 (mid-point)
Imperfect cadenceA comma: open, expectantEnd of phrase 1, to pull the ear toward what's next
Plagal / "Amen" cadenceA gentle, soft closeOccasionally as a final flourish

This is exactly why an A A' pair works so well: phrase A ends on a half cadence (comma), and A' reuses the same melodic shape but resolves onto a full cadence (full stop).

5. Quick Checklist Before You Start Writing

  • Decide how many lines your verse/section has — this usually decides your tune length (4 lines → 16 bars).
  • Sketch one good 4-bar phrase first. Make sure it's singable in a breath.
  • Decide: will line 2 contrast, or repeat-with-a-change?
  • Check each phrase ends with a clear sense of comma or full stop.
  • Look for a chance to bring phrase A back near the end (e.g. as line 4).
  • Sing it through. If you run out of breath, your phrase is too long.

6. Chords - in and outside the key

Once your phrases are mapped out, you need chords to support them. Every major key has seven "home" chords built from its scale.

The chords of a major key

Roman numeralChord qualityCommon nicknameTypical job
IMajorTonicHome. Where phrases resolve / start.
iiMinorSupertonicGentle movement away from home.
iiiMinorMediantSofter, more reflective colour.
IVMajorSubdominantOpens things up; common at phrase starts.
VMajorDominantCreates tension; wants to resolve to I.
viMinorSubmediantThe "relative minor" — wistful, warmer.
vii°DiminishedLeading-toneUnstable; rarely used alone, pulls strongly to I.
Rule of thumb: a satisfying hymn cadence is very often V → I (tension, then resolution) or IV → I (a softer, "Amen"-style close).

Borrowing chords: stepping outside the key

Contemporary hymn writers often reach beyond these seven "home" chords for a moment of colour or surprise. This is called modal mixture or borrowed chords: taking a chord from a closely related key and dropping it into your progression. It doesn't change the key permanently; it just tints one or two chords.

1. Borrowing from the parallel minor
Every major key has a "parallel minor" sharing the same home note (e.g. D major and D minor). You can borrow chords from it, such as the flattened VI (♭VI) and flattened III (♭III), or a minor iv instead of major IV. These give a sudden, slightly melancholy or "emotional" lean, often used to underline a serious lyric (loss, struggle, longing) before returning to the major key.
2. Borrowing from a closely related key
Keys that are "neighbours" on the circle of fifths (a fifth up or down from your home key) share most of their chords. Slipping in a chord from a neighbouring key, for instance using the V of V (a "secondary dominant"), adds a brief moment of harmonic lift. This is sometimes used as trick at the top of a chorus or going into a bridge.
3. Using the relative minor for the whole bridge or verse
Rather than borrowing single chords, some sections (often a bridge, as we have here) shift the centre of gravity toward the relative minor (vi) for several bars, giving a noticeably more serious or searching mood, before pulling back to the major key for a final chorus. This works well for text about struggle, courage, or waiting, before resolving into joy or certainty.

Where borrowed chords work best in a hymn

MomentWhy borrowing helps
Start of a bridgeSignals "something different is happening" without losing the listener
A line about struggle, darkness, or costA minor-borrowed chord can mirror the weight of the words
Just before the final chorusA secondary dominant can create lift and anticipation
A single dramatic wordOne borrowed chord under one word draws the ear to it
Workshop tip: use borrowed chords carefully; one or two per tune is usually enough. Too many, and the ear loses its sense of "home"; just the right amount, and it sounds like a deliberate, expressive choice.

7. William's Way

This is the hymn text our tune will carry. Notice the shape of each section, where the lines feel like "commas" and where they feel like "full stops," and where the structure might suggest repetition, contrast, or a key change.

Verse 1

We are gathered in your name, every story, every face;
You have called us, you have claimed us, in this bright and holy place.
So we open up our hands, and we open up our minds;
in the love of God we're learning, and it's hope that we will find.

Pre-Chorus

We will answer when you call,
one in heart and one in aim;
there is room enough for all,
in the shelter of Your name.

Chorus

Love of God and love of people,
Love of learning, love of life;
we will walk the Way before us,
faithful to Christ.
Deo Fidelis et Patriae,
with the Spirit as our guide;
You have named us, You have claimed us,
alive in you, alive.

Verse 2

Blessed William showed the way, when he stood and would not bend;
he was faithful to the Saviour, he was faithful to the end.
He had courage in the dark, and for justice he would stand;
he took up his cross and followed, with his life within God's hand.

Verse 3

At Your table we are fed, and we lift our hearts to You;
in the light of Christ we follow, walking close and walking true.
In our service, in our silence, in the labour and the praise;
for the gift of life you give us, we will love you all our days.

Bridge

Hope is rising, courage calling, justice for the poor;
service with our hands wide open, stillness at Your door.
This is William's Way before us, this the road we run;
following the Risen Jesus, until the journey's done.
